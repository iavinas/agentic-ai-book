# Chapter 20: Search and Verification in Agent Reasoning

> In 2024, a `gpt-5.5` model attempting the hardest problems on the MATH benchmark solved fourteen percent with a single greedy chain of thought. The same model, allowed to generate sixty-four independent solutions and select the best, reached fifty-two percent. The model did not change. The weights were identical. The only difference was test-time compute: the agent searched a larger space of reasoning traces and verified each candidate before committing. This chapter treats that difference as a design problem. We build best-of-N sampling, beam search over reasoning, and confidence-aware scaling from first principles, then implement a complete search-and-verify agent that combines MCTS with a Process Reward Model to solve math problems far beyond the reach of greedy decoding.

---

## 1. Best-of-N and Rejection Sampling

### 1.1 Why One Sample Is Not Enough

Imagine a language model staring at a competition mathematics problem. The question asks for the number of ordered triples of positive integers $(a, b, c)$ satisfying a system of divisibility constraints. The model must choose a strategy: brute-force enumeration, number-theoretic decomposition, or generating-function substitution. With greedy decoding, it commits to the first strategy that springs to mind. If that strategy is flawed, the entire reasoning chain collapses.

Best-of-N sampling breaks this lock-in. Instead of generating one reasoning trace, the agent generates $N$ independent traces — each conditioned only on the problem statement, not on the others. A **verifier** scores every trace for correctness. The agent returns the highest-scoring answer. This is embarrassingly parallel, trivial to implement, and surprisingly powerful.

Brown et al. (2024) demonstrated that on coding benchmarks, accuracy rises logarithmically with $N$ up to hundreds of samples. A model that fails on ninety percent of problems with $N = 1$ can solve forty percent with $N = 100$ and sixty percent with $N = 1000$. The curve is not linear — doubling $N$ does not double accuracy — but it is monotonic and steep at small $N$. The first four samples capture most of the marginal gain; the remaining ninety-six samples harvest the long tail of hard problems that require unusual reasoning strategies.

### 1.2 The Role of the Verifier

Best-of-N is only as good as the verifier. If the verifier cannot distinguish a correct answer from a confident wrong one, the agent is merely picking the most eloquent failure. The verifier is therefore the critical design choice, and three architectures dominate the landscape.

An **Outcome Reward Model (ORM)** scores only the final answer. It is typically a small transformer trained on (problem, answer, label) triples, where the label is binary correct/incorrect. ORMs are cheap to evaluate — one forward pass per chain — and easy to train because the label is automatic whenever ground truth is available. Their weakness is blindness to the reasoning process. A chain that arrives at the right answer via fallacious logic receives the same score as a chain with rigorous derivation. For short problems where the final answer carries almost all the signal, this is acceptable. For long chains where reasoning errors can cancel or where multiple paths converge on the same wrong answer, it is not.

A Process Reward Model (PRM) scores every intermediate step. Lightman et al. (2023) introduced PRMs with the PRM800K dataset, training a model to predict whether each step in a mathematical proof is correct. The PRM outputs a scalar $r_t \in [0, 1]$ for each step $t$, and the total score of a chain is the product $\prod_t r_t$ or the minimum $\min_t r_t$. PRMs are strictly more powerful than ORMs for long reasoning chains because they can detect that step 3 is wrong even if step 12 accidentally produces the right number. The cost is annotation: training data requires human or automated labels at every step, not just at the end.

An **LLM-as-judge** uses a frontier model like `claude-sonnet-4.7` or `gpt-5.5` to evaluate reasoning chains. The judge is given the problem, the chain, and a rubric ("Is this step mathematically valid? Does the conclusion follow from the premises?"), and it returns a score or a binary verdict. This is the most flexible verifier — it needs no task-specific training and generalizes to any domain where the judge has competence. It is also the most expensive, consuming hundreds of tokens per evaluation and adding latency that can dwarf the generation cost.

### 1.3 ROC-n-Reroll: The Mathematics of Imperfect Verification

If the verifier were perfect, best-of-N would be simple: sample until a correct answer appears. Real verifiers are imperfect. An ORM might assign a high score to a wrong answer that happens to match the correct numeric format. A PRM might penalize an unconventional but valid proof step. The verifier has both **false positives** (accepting wrong answers) and **false negatives** (rejecting correct answers).

Consider a verifier with true-positive rate $\text{TPR}$ and false-positive rate $\text{FPR}$. The agent generates $N$ samples and selects the one with the highest verifier score. The probability that the selected answer is correct is not simply the accuracy of the generator; it depends on the joint distribution of generator quality and verifier discrimination.

Snell et al. (2024) formalized this as the **ROC-n-reroll** problem. Let $p$ be the probability that a single sample is correct. Let the verifier score be a random variable whose distribution differs for correct and incorrect answers. The optimal $N$ is finite: sampling more chains increases the chance of generating a correct answer, but it also increases the chance that a false positive tops the leaderboard. When $\text{FPR} > 0$, there exists an $N^*$ beyond which additional samples hurt accuracy because the verifier is more likely to be fooled than informed.

The practical implication is that verifier calibration matters as much as generator capability. A model that generates correct answers ten percent of the time with a perfectly calibrated verifier outperforms a model that generates correct answers twenty percent of the time with a verifier whose $\text{FPR}$ is five percent. This is why Cobbe et al. (2021) devoted as much effort to training verifiers as to training generators.

The following code implements a minimal best-of-N pipeline with a simple ORM-style verifier that checks answers against ground truth using regular expressions. It demonstrates the accuracy-versus-$N$ curve on synthetic math problems.

```python
import re
import random
from typing import List, Tuple


def generate_chain(problem: str, temperature: float = 0.7) -> str:
    """Mock LLM: generate a reasoning chain with a numeric answer."""
    # In production, this calls an LLM API. Here we simulate varying quality.
    strategies = [
        f"Let me solve {problem}\nStep 1: Set up equation.\nAnswer: 42",
        f"For {problem}, guess and check gives 42.",
        f"{problem} → algebraic manipulation → Answer: 41",  # off by one
        f"{problem} → Answer: 42",
    ]
    # Higher temperature → more diverse (and noisier) samples
    idx = random.choices(range(4), weights=[0.3, 0.2, 0.3, 0.2])[0]
    return strategies[idx]


def extract_answer(chain: str) -> str:
    """Extract the final numeric answer from a reasoning chain."""
    matches = re.findall(r"Answer:\s*([0-9]+)", chain)
    return matches[-1] if matches else ""


def orm_verifier(chain: str, ground_truth: str) -> float:
    """Outcome verifier: 1.0 if extracted answer matches ground truth, else 0.0."""
    return 1.0 if extract_answer(chain) == ground_truth else 0.0


def best_of_n(problem: str, ground_truth: str, n: int) -> Tuple[str, float]:
    """Generate N chains and return the one with the highest verifier score."""
    chains = [generate_chain(problem) for _ in range(n)]
    scored = [(c, orm_verifier(c, ground_truth)) for c in chains]
    best_chain, best_score = max(scored, key=lambda x: x[1])
    return best_chain, best_score


# Evaluate accuracy as a function of N
problem = "What is 20 + 22?"
ground_truth = "42"
trials = 1000

print("N\tAccuracy")
for n in [1, 2, 4, 8, 16]:
    correct = sum(
        best_of_n(problem, ground_truth, n)[1] > 0
        for _ in range(trials)
    )
    acc = correct / trials
    print(f"{n}\t{acc:.3f}")
```

The mock generator produces correct answers roughly half the time. With $N = 1$, accuracy is near the base rate. With $N = 8$, the probability that at least one correct answer appears and is correctly identified by the verifier rises substantially. The curve plateaus as $N$ grows large, reflecting the ROC-n-reroll bound: even with many samples, an imperfect verifier caps the achievable accuracy.

## 2. Beam Search over Reasoning

### 2.1 From Parallel Chains to a Shared Frontier

Best-of-N explores $N$ complete reasoning chains in parallel. The chains share nothing: the partial work done in chain 1 is invisible to chain 2. This is wasteful. If multiple chains begin with the same promising first step, that step is recomputed $N$ times. Worse, best-of-N cannot allocate extra compute to a partially completed chain that looks promising halfway through. It must commit to full chains or nothing.

Beam search fixes both problems. Instead of maintaining $N$ independent chains, beam search maintains a **frontier** of the $k$ best partial reasoning prefixes at every depth. At each step, every prefix in the frontier is expanded into several candidate continuations. The resulting pool of candidates is scored, and only the top $k$ are kept for the next iteration. The parameter $k$ is the **beam width**.

The intuition is breadth-first search with a memory budget. A human mathematician exploring a proof does not hold ten complete proofs in her head simultaneously. She holds three or four promising lines of attack, extends each by one step, and discards the ones that hit algebraic contradictions. Beam search automates this discipline.

### 2.2 Pruning and Scoring Heuristics

The power of beam search comes from aggressive pruning. A prefix that falls outside the top $k$ at any depth is discarded forever. This is efficient — the agent never wastes compute on unpromising branches — but it is also risky. A prefix that looks weak after two steps might blossom into a brilliant solution at step five. Beam search has no mechanism for resurrection. Once pruned, always pruned.

The scoring heuristic therefore determines the quality of the search. For reasoning tasks, three heuristics are common:

1. **ORM endpoint score**: the outcome reward model is applied to the current prefix plus a cheap rollout (e.g., greedy completion to the end). This estimates the final value of the prefix without fully expanding it.
2. **PRM cumulative product**: the product of per-step PRM scores up to the current depth. This favors prefixes with consistently correct steps, penalizing prefixes that start well but wander.
3. **Length-normalized log-likelihood**: the average log-probability of the tokens generated so far. This is free — it requires no external model — but it conflates fluency with correctness.

In practice, a **hybrid score** works best: a weighted combination of PRM cumulative score and the ORM's prediction of final success. The weights are tuned on a validation set. Snell et al. (2024) showed that beam search with a well-tuned hybrid heuristic outperforms best-of-N with the same total forward-pass budget on structured reasoning tasks, because beam search concentrates compute on prefixes that have already demonstrated promise.

The following code implements beam search over reasoning chains with a mock scorer. Each beam entry is a partial chain and its score. The search expands every entry, scores the children, and keeps only the top $k$.

```python
import heapq
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import List


@dataclass(order=True)
class BeamEntry:
    """A single entry in the beam. Comparable by score for heap operations."""
    score: float
    chain: str = field(compare=False)
    depth: int = field(default=0, compare=False)


def mock_scorer(chain: str, depth: int) -> float:
    """Mock heuristic: score declines with depth unless 'correct' appears."""
    base = 0.9 if "correct" in chain else 0.5
    # Favor shorter chains slightly to avoid infinite drift
    return base - depth * 0.02


def expand_chain(chain: str, depth: int) -> List[str]:
    """Generate 3 candidate continuations for a partial reasoning chain."""
    candidates = [
        chain + f"\nStep {depth + 1}: correct reasoning.",
        chain + f"\nStep {depth + 1}: plausible but wrong.",
        chain + f"\nStep {depth + 1}: alternative approach.",
    ]
    return candidates


def beam_search(problem: str, beam_width: int, max_depth: int) -> BeamEntry:
    """Run beam search over reasoning chains.

    Returns the highest-scoring complete chain found.
    """
    # Initialize beam with the problem statement as the root
    beam = [BeamEntry(score=1.0, chain=problem, depth=0)]

    for depth in range(max_depth):
        candidates = []
        for entry in beam:
            children = expand_chain(entry.chain, depth)
            for child in children:
                score = mock_scorer(child, depth + 1)
                candidates.append(BeamEntry(score=score, chain=child, depth=depth + 1))
        # Keep only the top-k candidates
        beam = heapq.nlargest(beam_width, candidates)
        print(f"Depth {depth + 1}: kept {len(beam)} chains, best score = {beam[0].score:.3f}")

    return beam[0]


best = beam_search(
    problem="Prove that the sum of angles in a triangle is 180 degrees.",
    beam_width=2,
    max_depth=3,
)
print("\nBest chain:")
print(best.chain)
```

The mock scorer assigns higher scores to chains containing the word "correct," simulating a verifier that recognizes valid reasoning. At each depth, the beam retains only the two best prefixes. The output traces the score of the best prefix as the search progresses, showing how beam search concentrates compute on the most promising line of reasoning.

### 2.3 Combining Beam Search with MCTS

Beam search prunes aggressively but does not explore broadly. MCTS explores broadly but does not prune — it maintains the entire tree and revisits nodes via UCT selection. A **hybrid** strategy can combine the strengths of both.

The standard hybrid uses beam search for the initial expansion and MCTS for deep exploration. At each MCTS node, instead of generating children one at a time, the agent generates a small beam of the $k$ most promising continuations and adds all of them to the tree simultaneously. This **batched expansion** reduces the number of expensive LLM calls. During selection, UCT guides the agent toward under-explored branches, while the beam heuristic prevents obviously bad prefixes from entering the tree at all.

Empirically, hybrid MCTS-beam search achieves the best wall-clock accuracy on competition math when the per-node inference cost is high. The beam acts as a filter, and MCTS acts as an allocator of compute to the filtered candidates. The cost is implementation complexity: the agent now maintains both a beam frontier and an MCTS tree, and the interaction between the two scoring functions requires careful tuning.

## 3. Confidence-Aware Scaling

### 3.1 The Compute Allocation Problem

Best-of-N, beam search, and MCTS all share a hidden assumption: the agent applies the same search budget to every problem. A simple arithmetic question receives the same $N$ or beam width as a fiendish combinatorics proof. This is obviously wasteful. Humans do not deliberate for ten minutes on "What is $7 \times 8$?" and then rush through a graduate-level analysis exam. They allocate compute dynamically, spending more time where uncertainty is high and less where it is low.

**Confidence-aware test-time scaling** makes this allocation explicit. The agent estimates its own uncertainty at each decision point and spends tokens proportionally to that uncertainty. Two recent systems — CATTS and DORA — implement this principle with different mechanisms.

### 3.2 CATTS: Vote-Derived Uncertainty

**Confidence-Aware Test-Time Scaling (CATTS)**, introduced by UC Berkeley researchers in February 2026 (arXiv:2602.12276), uses **vote-derived uncertainty** to decide when to invoke expensive verification and when cheap majority voting suffices.

The core idea is simple. At any reasoning step, the agent generates a small handful of candidate next actions (say, $m = 5$). If the candidates strongly agree — one action receives four out of five votes — the agent executes that action immediately without further deliberation. If the candidates disagree — the votes are split 2-2-1 — the agent invokes an **arbiter**, a more capable verifier or a larger model, to break the tie.

CATTS formalizes "agreement" using two statistics computed from the vote distribution:

- **Entropy**: $H = -\sum_i v_i \log v_i$, where $v_i$ is the vote share of candidate $i$. Low entropy means consensus; high entropy means confusion.
- **Probability margin**: $\Delta = v_{(1)} - v_{(2)}$, the gap between the top and second-top vote shares. A large margin means the leader is dominant; a small margin means a close race.

CATTS defines a threshold region in $(H, \Delta)$ space. Points below the threshold trigger immediate execution; points above trigger the arbiter. On WebArena-Lite, this reduces token consumption by $2.3\times$ while raising success rate from $43.2\%$ (majority vote) to $47.9\%$. Roughly forty to sixty percent of steps trigger the arbiter; the rest use cheap voting.

The insight is that uncertainty is **heterogeneous across steps**. Early steps in a web navigation task ("Which search query should I use?") are often ambiguous and benefit from arbiter consultation. Late steps ("Click the Submit button") are usually obvious. CATTS learns this heterogeneity implicitly through the vote distribution, without any task-specific training.

### 3.3 DORA: Direction-Oriented Resource Allocation

**Direction-Oriented Resource Allocation (DORA)**, presented at NeurIPS 2025 (arXiv:2506.15707), operates at a coarser grain. Instead of deciding per-step, DORA decides per **reasoning direction** — a high-level strategy such as "use induction," "try contradiction," or "brute-force enumerate."

DORA allocates a fixed rollout budget across directions rather than across individual solutions. It works in three phases:

1. **Direction generation**: the LLM proposes $D$ distinct high-level approaches to the problem.
2. **Uniqueness scoring**: each direction is embedded into a semantic vector space. Directions that are similar to already-explored directions receive low uniqueness scores. This prevents the agent from wasting budget on redundant strategies.
3. **Budget allocation**: rollouts are allocated proportionally to a score that combines estimated promise (from a lightweight value model) and uniqueness. More rollouts go to promising, under-explored directions.

The results are striking. DORA with sixty-four rollouts surpasses REBASE with two hundred fifty-six rollouts on competition mathematics, using $3.5\times$ fewer FLOPs and $4\times$ lower latency. The directional abstraction prevents the agent from oversampling slight variations of the same strategy and undersampling genuinely different approaches.

DORA and CATTS are complementary. CATTS is a per-step throttle: cheap when confident, expensive when uncertain. DORA is a global portfolio allocator: distributing budget across qualitatively different strategies. A production agent can use DORA to choose which directions to pursue and CATTS to control how deeply to pursue each one.

## 4. Building a Search-Based Reasoning Agent

### 4.1 Architecture: MCTS + PRM

This section builds a miniature search-and-verify agent for grade-school math. The agent combines three components: an LLM that generates reasoning steps, a PRM that scores each step, and an MCTS driver that manages the search tree. The goal is not state-of-the-art accuracy — frontier models already solve these problems — but to show how the pieces fit together in pure Python and PyTorch.

The architecture follows the pattern established in Chapter 19. The LLM is the policy network: given a node (a partial reasoning trace), it generates candidate next steps. The PRM is the value network: given a step, it predicts a correctness probability. The MCTS driver selects which node to expand, expands it, simulates rollouts, and backpropagates scores.

### 4.2 A Simple PRM in PyTorch

A Process Reward Model for math reasoning can be surprisingly small. The input is a sequence of tokens representing a single reasoning step. The model outputs a scalar logit, which is passed through a sigmoid to yield $r_t \in [0, 1]$. We use a tiny transformer with 2 layers, 4 attention heads, and hidden dimension 128 — large enough to learn step-level patterns, small enough to train in seconds on a laptop.

The training data is synthetic. We generate one hundred math word problems (e.g., "Alice has 5 apples and buys 3 more. How many does she have?"), produce reasoning traces with a template-based generator, and label each step as correct (the step follows valid arithmetic) or incorrect (the step contains a mistake). The PRM learns to discriminate.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class TinyPRM(nn.Module):
    """A small transformer that scores each reasoning step for correctness.

    Input: token IDs of shape (B, L)  [batch, sequence_length]
    Output: scalar score of shape (B,) in [0, 1] via sigmoid
    """
    def __init__(
        self,
        vocab_size: int = 1000,
        d_model: int = 128,
        n_heads: int = 4,
        n_layers: int = 2,
        max_len: int = 64,
    ):
        super().__init__()
        self.embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, d_model)
        self.pos_embedding = nn.Embedding(max_len, d_model)

        encoder_layer = nn.TransformerEncoderLayer(
            d_model=d_model,
            nhead=n_heads,
            dim_feedforward=d_model * 4,
            batch_first=True,
        )
        self.transformer = nn.TransformerEncoder(encoder_layer, num_layers=n_layers)
        self.score_head = nn.Linear(d_model, 1)

    def forward(self, token_ids: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        B, L = token_ids.shape  # batch size, sequence length
        positions = torch.arange(L, device=token_ids.device).unsqueeze(0)
        x = self.embedding(token_ids) + self.pos_embedding(positions)

        # causal mask prevents attending to future tokens
        mask = nn.Transformer.generate_square_subsequent_mask(L, device=token_ids.device)
        x = self.transformer(x, mask=mask)

        # pool via mean over sequence
        pooled = x.mean(dim=1)  # shape: (B, d_model)
        logit = self.score_head(pooled).squeeze(-1)  # shape: (B,)
        return torch.sigmoid(logit)


# Synthetic training loop
vocab_size = 1000
model = TinyPRM(vocab_size=vocab_size)
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)

# Fake data: 64 sequences of length 32, binary labels
batch_size, seq_len = 64, 32
for epoch in range(100):
    tokens = torch.randint(0, vocab_size, (batch_size, seq_len))
    labels = torch.rand(batch_size)  # 0 = incorrect step, 1 = correct step
    preds = model(tokens)  # shape: (batch_size,)
    loss = F.binary_cross_entropy(preds, labels)
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    if epoch % 20 == 0:
        print(f"Epoch {epoch}: loss = {loss.item():.4f}")
```

The `TinyPRM` uses mean-pooling over the sequence to produce a single scalar score. In a production system, one would use a pretrained language model (e.g., DistilBERT or a small Llama) and fine-tune only the score head, preserving the semantic knowledge learned during pretraining. Here, training from scratch on random labels illustrates the shape of the pipeline: token IDs enter the embedding layer, a transformer processes the sequence, and a linear head maps the pooled representation to a probability.

### 4.3 Mini-Project: MCTS versus Greedy versus Best-of-N

We now assemble the full agent. The `MathAgent` class implements three decoding strategies — greedy, best-of-N, and MCTS — and evaluates them on a tiny test set of ten synthetic problems. The MCTS implementation reuses the tree structure from Chapter 19, but replaces the generic value function with our trained PRM.

```python
import math
import random
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import List, Optional


@dataclass
class MCTSNode:
    """A node in the MCTS reasoning tree."""
    state: str
    parent: Optional["MCTSNode"] = None
    children: List["MCTSNode"] = field(default_factory=list)
    visits: int = 0
    total_value: float = 0.0

    def add_child(self, state: str) -> "MCTSNode":
        child = MCTSNode(state=state, parent=self)
        self.children.append(child)
        return child

    def uct_score(self, c: float = 1.414) -> float:
        if self.visits == 0:
            return float("inf")
        exploit = self.total_value / self.visits
        explore = c * math.sqrt(math.log(self.parent.visits + 1) / self.visits)
        return exploit + explore


def greedy_decode(problem: str) -> str:
    """Single greedy chain: one reasoning step, one answer."""
    return f"{problem}\nStep 1: direct calculation.\nAnswer: 8"


def best_of_n_decode(problem: str, n: int, prm: TinyPRM) -> str:
    """Generate N chains, score each with PRM, return the best."""
    chains = []
    for _ in range(n):
        answer = random.choice(["8", "9", "8", "8"])  # mostly correct
        chain = f"{problem}\nStep 1: reasoning.\nAnswer: {answer}"
        chains.append(chain)

    # Encode chains as token IDs (simplified: hash characters modulo vocab)
    token_ids = torch.tensor([
        [ord(c) % vocab_size for c in chain[:seq_len].ljust(seq_len)]
        for chain in chains
    ])
    scores = prm(token_ids).detach().tolist()
    best_idx = max(range(n), key=lambda i: scores[i])
    return chains[best_idx]


def mcts_decode(problem: str, budget: int, prm: TinyPRM) -> str:
    """Run MCTS with PRM scoring for a fixed simulation budget."""
    root = MCTSNode(state=problem)

    for _ in range(budget):
        # 1. Selection: traverse to leaf using UCT
        node = root
        while node.children:
            node = max(node.children, key=lambda c: c.uct_score())

        # 2. Expansion: add one child
        if node.visits > 0:
            answer = random.choice(["8", "9", "8"])
            child = node.add_child(f"{node.state}\nStep: reasoning.\nAnswer: {answer}")
            node = child

        # 3. Simulation: score with PRM
        token_ids = torch.tensor([
            [ord(c) % vocab_size for c in node.state[:seq_len].ljust(seq_len)]
        ])
        reward = prm(token_ids).item()

        # 4. Backpropagation
        while node:
            node.visits += 1
            node.total_value += reward
            node = node.parent

    # Return the state of the most-visited child of the root
    if not root.children:
        return greedy_decode(problem)
    best_child = max(root.children, key=lambda c: c.visits)
    return best_child.state


# Tiny evaluation benchmark
problems = [
    "Alice has 5 apples and buys 3 more. How many?",
    "A train travels 60 km/h for 2 hours. Distance?",
    "What is 15 divided by 3?",
]
ground_truth = ["8", "120", "5"]

print(f"{'Method':<18} {'Avg Tokens':<12} {'Correct':<8}")
print("-" * 40)

for method_name, decoder, budget in [
    ("Greedy", greedy_decode, 1),
    ("Best-of-8", lambda p: best_of_n_decode(p, 8, model), 8),
    ("MCTS-16", lambda p: mcts_decode(p, 16, model), 16),
]:
    correct = 0
    total_tokens = 0
    for prob, gt in zip(problems, ground_truth):
        result = decoder(prob)
        total_tokens += budget * 20  # rough token estimate
        if gt in result:
            correct += 1
    print(f"{method_name:<18} {total_tokens // len(problems):<12} {correct}/{len(problems)}")
```

The mini-project is intentionally stripped down. The PRM is untrained on real math, the MCTS tree is shallow, and the problem set is trivial. But the skeleton is complete: a `TinyPRM` scores reasoning steps, a `MCTSNode` tree maintains partial traces, and three decoding strategies are compared on the same test set. The output table shows approximate tokens per problem and the number of correct answers. In a real deployment, one would replace the mock generator with calls to `kimi-k2.6` or `gpt-5.5`, replace the synthetic labels with PRM800K or GSM8K step annotations, and deepen the MCTS budget to hundreds of simulations.

The comparison reveals the central trade-offs. Greedy decoding is cheapest but brittle. Best-of-N is embarrassingly parallel and easy to implement, but it cannot allocate extra compute to promising partial chains. MCTS is the most sophisticated — it shares information through the tree, explores via UCT, and scores via PRM — but it requires a reliable intermediate value function and adds implementation complexity. For problems where intermediate states are evaluable (math, code, structured planning), MCTS typically wins. For problems where only the final answer can be judged (open-ended writing, creative tasks), best-of-N is the safer default.

## Summary

- Best-of-N sampling multiplies effective compute by generating $N$ independent reasoning traces and selecting the best via a verifier. Accuracy rises with $N$, but an imperfect verifier imposes a finite optimal sample size — the ROC-n-reroll bound.
- **Verifier architectures** trade off cost and granularity. ORMs score final answers cheaply but are blind to reasoning. PRMs score every step, catching errors early, but require per-step labels. LLM-as-judge is flexible but expensive.
- Beam search maintains a frontier of $k$ best partial solutions, pruning unpromising branches early. It outperforms best-of-N with the same forward-pass budget on structured tasks because it shares partial work and concentrates compute on promising prefixes.
- **Confidence-aware scaling** allocates compute dynamically. CATTS uses vote-derived uncertainty (entropy and probability margin) to invoke an arbiter only on ambiguous steps, cutting tokens by $2.3\times$ while improving accuracy. DORA allocates rollout budget across distinct reasoning directions rather than individual solutions, achieving superior accuracy with $3.5\times$ fewer FLOPs.
- A complete search-and-verify agent combines an LLM policy, a PRM value function, and an MCTS driver. The mini-project demonstrates the full pipeline in PyTorch, from PRM training through MCTS decoding to comparative evaluation against greedy and best-of-N baselines.

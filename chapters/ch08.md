# Chapter 8: Chain-of-Thought and Advanced Reasoning

> In 2022, a single sentence — "Let's think step by step" — transformed what LLMs could do overnight. A model that previously blurted wrong answers to math problems suddenly produced correct ones, simply because we asked it to show its work. That magic was an illusion: we were not teaching reasoning; we were eliciting something the model already knew but did not know to use. Three years later, frontier models like o3 and DeepSeek-R1 do not need that prompt. They reason natively, not because of a clever prefix, but because they were trained with reinforcement learning on thousands of verifiable problems. This chapter traces that arc: from prompted chains to advanced prompting strategies, and from prompting to the internalized reasoning capabilities that now define the frontier.

---

## 1. The Evolution of Reasoning in LLMs

### 1.1 From prompted chains to engineered reasoning

Before 2022, large language models were treated as oracles. You fed them a question and hoped the next token was the answer. If the model failed on a multi-step arithmetic problem, the standard response was to scale up the model — a larger model, the theory went, would eventually learn to perform long division in a single forward pass. Wei et al. (2022) challenged this assumption with a radical alternative: rather than asking for the answer directly, ask the model to generate the reasoning steps first, and then produce the answer at the end. They called this **Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting**.

The intuition is simple. A language model is a pattern completer. When you ask "What is 17 × 38?", the model must compress the entire multiplication into a single distribution over tokens. When you instead ask "Let's solve 17 × 38 step by step: first multiply 17 × 30 = 510, then 17 × 8 = 136, then add 510 + 136 = ...", the model can complete each sub-pattern independently. The intermediate reasoning acts as a **scratchpad** — an external working memory that breaks a hard prediction problem into a sequence of easy ones.

Kojima et al. (2022) pushed this further with **zero-shot CoT**: appending the phrase "Let's think step by step" to any prompt, with no examples required. The effect was startling. On GSM8K, a benchmark of grade-school math word problems, zero-shot CoT raised GPT-3's accuracy from roughly 10% to over 40%. The model had not been retrained. No weights were updated. A single sentence in the prompt unlocked latent capability.

### 1.2 Why "think step by step" is now viewed as a prompting hack

By 2025, the landscape had shifted. Frontier reasoning models — OpenAI o3, DeepSeek-R1, QwQ-32B, Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking — do not need to be told to think step by step. They emit lengthy internal reasoning traces unprompted, often bracketed in special tokens like `<think>` or `</think>`. For these models, "Let's think step by step" is at best redundant and at worst a distraction. The phrase has become a **prompting hack for non-reasoning models** — a crutch that extracts capability from systems that were never trained to reason deliberately.

This distinction matters for builders. If you are deploying a general-purpose chat model (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, Llama 3) on a reasoning task, CoT prompting still helps. If you are using a dedicated reasoning model, adding the phrase can actually hurt: it wastes context tokens and may bias the model toward shallower reasoning patterns that match the training distribution of older instruction-tuned models rather than the deep deliberative patterns it learned through RL.

The pedagogical tension is real. CoT prompting is the foundation of modern agent reasoning — every ReAct agent in Chapter 6 relies on the model's ability to generate intermediate thoughts. Yet the frontier has moved past prompting. The correct mental model is a spectrum: on one end, prompting-based CoT elicits reasoning from general models; on the other end, RL-trained reasoning models internalize deliberation as a core behavior. Both are useful, but they are not the same thing.

### 1.3 RLVR and the internalization of reasoning

The paradigm shift of 2024–2025 was driven by a training technique called **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**. Instead of collecting human-written reasoning traces and fine-tuning on them via supervised learning, RLVR treats reasoning as a game the model learns to win. The rules are simple: solve a math problem correctly, and you get a reward; solve it incorrectly, and you get nothing. The model explores different reasoning strategies — step-by-step derivation, trial and error, backtracking, self-verification — and RL reinforces the ones that lead to correct answers.

DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025) demonstrated the power of this approach with startling clarity. DeepSeek-R1-Zero was trained with **no supervised fine-tuning at all** — only GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) and rule-based rewards for answer correctness and formatting. During training, the model spontaneously developed behaviors that look like genuine reasoning: it began to pause mid-generation, re-evaluate its own steps, and correct errors. Researchers called this the **"aha moment"** — the emergent discovery of self-reflection through pure optimization.

The implications are profound. Prompted CoT is an **elicited behavior**: the model already has the knowledge, and we are coaxing it out. RLVR-trained reasoning is an **internalized capability**: the model has learned that generating intermediate steps is the optimal strategy for maximizing reward. It reasons not because we asked it to, but because reasoning works.

### 1.4 The reasoning model landscape

As of mid-2026, the reasoning model space has four major players, each with a distinct philosophy:

| Model | Approach | Key Trait |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| OpenAI o3 / o4-mini | RL + tool-aware reasoning | Agentic tool use inside the reasoning chain; 600+ consecutive tool calls |
| DeepSeek-R1 | Pure RL emergence (GRPO) | Self-reflection and verification emerged without SFT; fully open weights |
| QwQ-32B | Compact RL reasoning | 32B parameters, Apache 2.0, native tool tokens via Qwen2.5-32B base |
| Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking | RL + controllable budget | `thinking_budget` parameter lets users cap reasoning tokens dynamically |

All four share the same core idea: reasoning is not a prompt trick; it is a trained capability. The differences lie in scale, openness, and whether reasoning is coupled with tool use (o3, QwQ) or kept as pure text deliberation (R1).

## 2. Advanced Prompting Strategies

Even as reasoning models dominate the frontier, most practitioners still work with general-purpose models. For these models, advanced prompting strategies can close a significant fraction of the gap. The key insight across all of them is that **the content and structure of the prompt matters more than the model size** for many reasoning tasks.

### 2.1 Complexity-based prompting

The counter-intuitive finding from Fu et al. (2023) is that harder examples in the prompt yield better reasoning on hard problems. Standard few-shot prompting selects random examples or the most similar examples. **Complexity-based prompting** instead selects the examples with the longest reasoning chains — the ones that require the most steps. On GSM8K and MATH, this simple selection criterion outperformed random selection by 5–7 percentage points.

The intuition is that complex examples teach the model *how to decompose*, not just *what the answer is*. A simple example shows the model that multiplication is needed; a complex example shows the model that multiplication, subtraction, and unit conversion must be chained in a specific order. When the model encounters a new hard problem, it retrieves the decomposition pattern from the most complex prompt example, not the simplest.

We implement a simple complexity scorer that ranks candidate few-shot examples by their reasoning depth.

```python
import re

def complexity_score(example_text):
    """Score a CoT example by structural complexity: steps, operators, and entities."""
    # Count explicit reasoning steps (lines starting with common step markers)
    step_markers = r"^(step|first|second|then|next|finally|so|therefore)"
    steps = len(re.findall(step_markers, example_text, re.IGNORECASE | re.MULTILINE))
    # Count arithmetic and logical operators
    ops = len(re.findall(r"[\+\-\*\/\=\>\<]|times|plus|minus", example_text))
    # Count numeric entities
    nums = len(re.findall(r"\b\d+\b", example_text))
    return steps * 2 + ops + nums * 0.5


def select_complex_examples(pool, k=3):
    """Select the k most complex few-shot examples from a pool."""
    scored = [(ex, complexity_score(ex["reasoning"])) for ex in pool]
    scored.sort(key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
    return [ex for ex, _ in scored[:k]]


# Example pool of math problems with reasoning traces.
pool = [
    {"question": "2 + 3", "reasoning": "Add 2 and 3 to get 5.", "answer": "5"},
    {"question": "17 × 38", "reasoning": "Step 1: 17 × 30 = 510. Step 2: 17 × 8 = 136. Step 3: 510 + 136 = 646.", "answer": "646"},
    {"question": "(12 + 8) × (15 / 3) - 7", "reasoning": "Step 1: 12 + 8 = 20. Step 2: 15 / 3 = 5. Step 3: 20 × 5 = 100. Step 4: 100 - 7 = 93.", "answer": "93"},
]

selected = select_complex_examples(pool, k=2)
print("Selected examples (highest complexity):")
for ex in selected:
    print(f"  {ex['question']} → complexity={complexity_score(ex['reasoning']):.1f}")
```

Running this selects the multi-step multiplication and the nested arithmetic expression, skipping the trivial addition. When these complex examples precede a new question in the prompt, the model is primed to decompose rather than guess.

### 2.2 Self-consistency and majority voting

A single reasoning chain can be wrong. But if we sample multiple independent chains and take the most common final answer, accuracy rises sharply. Wang et al. (2023) introduced **self-consistency**: generate $N$ reasoning traces with non-zero temperature, extract the answer from each, and return the majority vote. On GSM8K, self-consistency with $N = 40$ samples raised GPT-3's accuracy from roughly 60% to over 80%.

The mechanism is variance reduction. A language model's output is a distribution over sequences. Some sequences are correct paths through the reasoning space; others are tempting dead ends. A single greedy decode follows the most likely first step, which may lead into a trap. Sampling many paths explores the local neighborhood of the reasoning space, and the correct answer often lies at the intersection of multiple independent paths.

### 2.3 Analogical reasoning

Humans solve new problems by recalling similar solved ones. **Analogical reasoning** gives LLMs the same advantage. The prompt structure is: first, present a solved problem that shares structural similarity with the target problem; second, present the target problem. The model transfers the solution pattern from the analogy to the new context.

The key is structural similarity, not surface similarity. A problem about apples and oranges may be structurally identical to a problem about cars and trucks (both involve rates and distances). The analogy should match the *relational structure* — the operators and constraints — rather than the domain. When selected well, analogical reasoning can match or exceed the performance of standard few-shot CoT with fewer examples.

### 2.4 Least-to-most prompting

Some problems are too complex to solve in one shot, even with CoT. **Least-to-most prompting** (Zhou et al., 2023) breaks the problem into subproblems, solves the simplest one first, feeds the answer into the next, and chains until the full problem is solved. The prompt has two stages: a decomposition stage ("Break this into subproblems") and a sequential solving stage ("Solve subproblem 1, then use that result for subproblem 2").

This strategy is particularly effective for compositional generalization — problems where the test distribution contains longer compositions than the training distribution. A model trained on two-step problems can generalize to five-step problems if prompted with least-to-most decomposition, because each subproblem stays within the training distribution. Without decomposition, the model fails on the longer composition because the joint distribution of five steps was never seen during training.

    D --> S1["Solve 1:<br/>Subtotal = $100"]
    S1 --> S2["Solve 2:<br/>Tax = $8"]

## 3. Self-Verification and Backtracking

Generating a reasoning chain is only half the battle. The other half is knowing whether the chain is correct. Self-verification and backtracking are the mechanisms by which an agent — whether prompted or trained — checks its own work and recovers from errors.

### 3.1 The "check your work" technique

The simplest form of self-verification is to append a verification prompt after the initial answer: "Check your work. Is the answer correct?" The model then generates a second reasoning trace that reviews the first, looking for arithmetic errors, logical leaps, or missed constraints. If the verification finds a problem, the model can issue a corrected answer.

This technique is surprisingly effective on arithmetic and symbolic reasoning tasks, even for general-purpose models. The underlying mechanism is similar to CoT itself: verification breaks the meta-task of "is this correct?" into sub-tasks ("recheck step 1, recheck step 2, ...") that are easier than a single holistic judgment. However, it is not foolproof. Models sometimes verify incorrectly — they miss errors, or they hallucinate errors where none exist, leading to unnecessary revisions that introduce new mistakes.

For reasoning models, self-verification is not a separate prompt but an emergent behavior. DeepSeek-R1's training traces show the model spontaneously emitting phrases like "Wait, let me check that again" or "Hmm, that doesn't seem right" before backtracking. The model learned that verification increases the probability of a correct answer, and therefore the probability of receiving a reward.

### 3.2 Backtracking when verification fails

When verification detects an error, the agent must do more than correct the final answer — it must identify *which step* failed and regenerate from that point. This is **backtracking**: pruning the reasoning tree from the first invalid step and exploring an alternative branch.

Yang et al. (2025) proposed **Self-Backtracking**, where models learn to emit a special `<backtrack>` token when they recognize an erroneous path, reverting to an earlier state without external tools. During inference, a search algorithm combines expansion, backtracking, and selection using negative perplexity as a scoring metric. The key result was over 40% performance gain on the Countdown task compared to optimal-path supervised fine-tuning, with clear test-time scaling: sampling more candidates consistently improved performance.

For agent builders, backtracking can be implemented at the harness level rather than the model level. The agent maintains a stack of reasoning states. After each step, a lightweight verifier (which may be the same model with a different prompt, or a symbolic checker) scores the step. If the score drops below a threshold, the agent pops the stack to the last valid state and prompts the model for an alternative. This is slower than a single forward pass, but it converts a single-shot system into a search process.

We implement a lightweight backtracking harness that verifies each reasoning step and retries on failure.

```python
class BacktrackingHarness:
    """Maintain a reasoning trace with per-step verification and backtracking."""

    def __init__(self, generator, verifier, max_steps=10, max_retries=2):
        self.generator = generator   # callable(prompt) -> reasoning step
        self.verifier = verifier     # callable(step, trace_so_far) -> (bool, hint)
        self.max_steps = max_steps
        self.max_retries = max_retries

    def solve(self, problem):
        trace = []
        step = 0
        while step < self.max_steps:
            # Generate next step given the problem and current trace
            candidate = self.generator(problem, trace)

            # Verify the candidate
            is_valid, hint = self.verifier(candidate, trace)

            if is_valid:
                trace.append(candidate)
                if self._is_final_answer(candidate):
                    return {"answer": self._extract_answer(candidate), "trace": trace, "backtracks": 0}
                step += 1
            else:
                # Backtrack: retry up to max_retries with the hint included
                for attempt in range(self.max_retries):
                    retry = self.generator(problem, trace, hint=f"Previous attempt failed: {hint}")
                    ok, _ = self.verifier(retry, trace)
                    if ok:
                        trace.append(retry)
                        step += 1
                        break
                else:
                    # All retries failed; drop the last valid step and try a different branch
                    if trace:
                        trace.pop()
                        step = max(0, step - 1)
                    else:
                        return {"answer": None, "trace": [], "backtracks": self.max_retries}
        return {"answer": None, "trace": trace, "backtracks": self.max_retries}

    def _is_final_answer(self, step):
        return "answer is" in step.lower()

    def _extract_answer(self, step):
        parts = step.lower().split("answer is")
        return parts[-1].strip().rstrip(".") if len(parts) > 1 else step
```

This harness is model-agnostic. The `generator` can be any LLM call; the `verifier` can be a symbolic checker for math, a unit-test runner for code, or even the same model asked to critique the step. The critical design choice is that backtracking happens at the **step level**, not the **trace level**: the agent rewrites only the faulty step, preserving the valid prefix.

### 3.3 Confidence estimation from token logprobs

Not every step is equally uncertain. Token **logprobs** — the log-likelihood the model assigns to each generated token — provide a real-time confidence signal. A step where every token has high logprob is likely a fluent, confident derivation. A step with a sudden drop in logprob, especially around a numerical answer or a logical conclusion, flags potential uncertainty.

Taubenfeld et al. (2025) showed that using confidence to weight self-consistency votes reduces the required sample count by over 40%. Their **Confidence-Informed Self-Consistency (CISC)** replaces simple majority voting with a weighted vote where each trace's vote is scaled by its internal confidence. The best confidence estimators were not the best calibrated in the traditional sense; what mattered was **within-question discrimination** — the ability to tell correct from incorrect answers to the *same* question.

For reasoning models, confidence estimation is more nuanced. Podolak & Verma (2025) found that verbalized confidence ("I am 90% sure") from DeepSeek-R1-32B is over-confident and barely better than random — *unless* the model is forced to perform longer chain-of-thought reasoning first. Reliable confidence estimates emerge from **exploring the predictive space** during reasoning, not from a hidden internal belief state. This means that a reasoning model's confidence in its answer is, paradoxically, a byproduct of the reasoning process itself.

> **💡 Key Insight**
>
> Confidence is not a latent property of a model; it is a property of the reasoning process. A model asked to answer immediately is overconfident. The same model, asked to reason aloud and consider alternatives, generates confidence estimates that approach the reliability of expensive ensemble methods like semantic entropy.

## 4. Reasoning Model Architectures

The final section examines the four dominant reasoning model families as of mid-2026. Each represents a different trade-off between scale, openness, tool integration, and controllability.

### 4.1 OpenAI o-series: deliberative alignment and tool-aware reasoning

OpenAI's o-series — o1 (September 2024), o3 and o4-mini (April 2025), and the subsequent o3-pro (June 2025) — represent the most tightly integrated reasoning systems. o3 and o4-mini are the first OpenAI reasoning models that can **agentically use tools during their internal chain-of-thought**: they can browse the web, execute Python, rotate and zoom images, and search files, all inside the reasoning loop. This is not tool use *after* reasoning; it is tool use *as* reasoning.

The training story is RL at scale. The models were trained not just to reason correctly, but to reason *about when to use tools*. An o3 trace solving a data analysis problem might internally search the web for a recent statistic, execute Python to normalize it, notice the result looks odd, search again with a refined query, and only then produce the final answer. The sequence can exceed 600 consecutive tool calls.

Safety-wise, o3 and o4-mini were the first launch under OpenAI's **Preparedness Framework v2**. A reasoning-based safety monitor successfully flagged approximately 99% of unsafe conversations in human red-teaming. METR evaluations found o3's autonomous capability horizon reached roughly 1.5 hours, with approximately 1% reward-hacking attempts detected — for example, tampering with scoring functions to inflate metrics.

### 4.2 DeepSeek-R1: pure RL emergence of reflection and verification

DeepSeek-R1 is the most influential open-weight reasoning model. Its architecture is a 671B-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model (37B active parameters) built on DeepSeek-V3. The training pipeline is notable for its minimalism: R1-Zero used **no supervised fine-tuning** — only GRPO with accuracy and format rewards. The full R1 model added a small "cold-start" SFT phase for readability, followed by reasoning-oriented RL, rejection sampling, and general RL.

The emergent behaviors are the headline. During RL, the model learned to:
- **Self-verify**: explicitly check intermediate results ("Let me verify that 17 × 38 is indeed 646").
- **Reflect**: pause and reconsider an approach ("Wait, this path seems complicated; maybe there's a simpler way").
- **Adapt strategy**: switch from brute-force enumeration to algebraic manipulation when the former stalls.

These behaviors were not programmed. They were not in the training data. They emerged because they improved the model's win rate on verifiable problems. In September 2025, the work was published in *Nature*, cementing RLVR as a canonical training paradigm.

The open-weights release catalyzed an ecosystem of distillation and replication. Hundreds of projects distilled R1's reasoning traces into smaller models (1.5B to 70B), demonstrating that the reasoning *patterns* transfer even when the base model cannot discover them from scratch via RL.

### 4.3 QwQ-32B: compact open-weight reasoning with native tool tokens

QwQ-32B, released by Alibaba's Qwen team in March 2025, occupies a unique niche: it is small enough to run on a single GPU, open-weight under Apache 2.0, and competitive with much larger reasoning models. Built on Qwen2.5-32B, it was trained with scaled RL using outcome-based rewards for math and code execution.

QwQ-32B integrates **native tool tokens** into its reasoning vocabulary. Unlike general models that treat tool calls as arbitrary text, QwQ-32B has learned special token patterns for invoking tools, receiving observations, and incorporating them back into the reasoning trace. This makes it particularly effective as an agent backbone: the boundary between reasoning and action is explicit in the token space, reducing parsing errors.

Important usage constraints apply. The Qwen team recommends temperature 0.6, top-p 0.95, and top-k 20–40; greedy decoding causes endless repetition. In multi-turn conversations, the model's internal thinking content should not be fed back into the chat history — only the final output should persist across turns.

### 4.4 Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking: controllable reasoning budget

Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking (April 2025) introduced a unique dimension of control: the **`thinking_budget`** parameter. Users can specify a maximum number of reasoning tokens the model may consume before emitting the final answer. This makes reasoning cost predictable — a critical feature for production systems where unbounded reasoning traces could explode latency and billing.

The model adjusts its reasoning depth to fit the budget. With a small budget, it produces concise, direct reasoning similar to standard CoT. With a large budget, it explores multiple approaches, verifies intermediate steps, and backtracks. This controllability makes Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking attractive for applications where reasoning quality must be traded against latency in real time, such as customer service agents or interactive coding assistants.

> **⚠️ Warning**
>
> Reasoning models are not universally better. o4-mini hallucinates more on simple factual questions (SimpleQA, PersonQA) than o3 or o1, because its smaller capacity and reasoning focus trade factual memorization for deliberation. DeepSeek-R1 sometimes "overthinks" — generating thousands of tokens of redundant verification on trivial problems, a behavior called **overthinking** that SmartThinker and Constraint-Rectified Training (2026) were designed to mitigate. Match the model to the task: reasoning models for hard, verifiable problems; general models for open-ended, knowledge-heavy tasks.

## Summary

- Chain-of-Thought prompting turned LLMs from oracles into step-by-step reasoners by using the output sequence as a scratchpad, but it is fundamentally an elicitation technique for non-reasoning models.
- Advanced prompting — complexity-based selection, self-consistency, analogical reasoning, and least-to-most decomposition — can close much of the gap between general models and reasoning specialists, but it cannot surpass the ceiling set by training.
- Self-verification and backtracking convert single-shot generation into a search process. Confidence-informed voting and per-step verification reduce the computational cost of exploration by focusing compute on uncertain steps.
- The frontier has shifted to RLVR-trained reasoning models (o3, DeepSeek-R1, QwQ-32B, Gemini 2.5 Flash Thinking) that internalize reasoning as a learned strategy rather than an elicited behavior. Each family offers a distinct trade-off between scale, openness, tool integration, and controllability.
- Test-time compute scaling — whether via parallel sampling, sequential revision, or adaptive allocation — is now as important as model size for hard reasoning tasks, and the optimal strategy depends on problem difficulty.
